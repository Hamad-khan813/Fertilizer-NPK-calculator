# Foliar Spray Application Guide & Calculator

Foliar feeding is one of the most efficient ways to deliver nutrients to a plant, especially when soil conditions make nutrient uptake difficult (such as high pH or cold temperatures). By spraying a nutrient solution directly onto the leaves, you bypass the soil entirely, allowing for almost immediate absorption.

However, foliar spraying is a "high risk, high reward" strategy. If your concentration is too high, you can cause devastating **Leaf Burn** (necrosis). If it's too low, you're wasting time and water.

In this guide, we'll focus on the most common foliar nutrient—**Urea**—and how to apply it safely.

---

## Why Foliar Feed?

While roots are the primary way plants drink, leaves have tiny pores called **Stomata** and a permeable cuticle that can absorb water-soluble nutrients.

- **Speed**: Nutrients can be absorbed in as little as 30 minutes.
- **Precision**: You can correct specific deficiencies (like Iron or Zinc) quickly.
- **Efficiency**: Near 90-100% of the nutrient is utilized, compared to as low as 10-20% in some soil applications.

---

## How Much Urea Per Liter? (The Golden Rule)

Urea (46-0-0) is the most popular foliar nitrogen source because it is a small, uncharged molecule that passes through the leaf cuticle more easily than nitrates or ammonium.

### Safe Concentration Rates
For most agricultural crops (tomatoes, peppers, corn, wheat):
- **Safe Range**: 0.5% to 1.0% solution.
- **In Grams**: 5g to 10g of Urea per 1 liter of water.

### Sensitive Plants
For sensitive plants (orchids, seedlings, or flowering ornamentals):
- **Safe Range**: 0.25% solution.
- **In Grams**: 2.5g of Urea per 1 liter of water.

### High-Volume Application
If you are doing a large-scale application, a common rate is **5 to 10 kg of Urea per 1000 liters** of water per hectare.

**Warning**: Never exceed a 2% concentration (20g/L) unless you are an expert grower with specific variety data. Most leaf burn occurs at the 2.5% threshold.

---

## The Best Time to Spray

Timing is everything in foliar application. The goal is to keep the leaves wet for as long as possible to maximize absorption time.

1.  **Early Morning (Best)**: The stomata are open, temperatures are cool, and humidity is usually higher.
2.  **Late Evening**: Good, but can lead to fungal issues if the leaves stay wet all night in humid climates.
3.  **Cloudy Days**: Excellent for foliar spraying as the solution won't evaporate quickly.

**NEVER spray in the middle of a hot, sunny day.** The water will evaporate instantly, leaving a highly concentrated salt crust on the leaf that will suck the moisture out of the plant cells, causing immediate burn.

---

## Step-by-Step Mixing Instructions

1.  **Check Water Quality**: Use clean water. High-pH or very hard water can reduce nutrient solubility.
2.  **Measure Accurately**: Use a digital scale. Guessing "a tablespoon" is how leaf burn happens.
3.  **Dissolve Fully**: Urea is highly soluble. Add it to a small amount of warm water first to create a concentrate, then dilute to final volume.
4.  **Add a Surfactant (Optional but Recommended)**: A tiny drop of dish soap or a professional "spreader-sticker" breaks the surface tension of the water. This prevents the spray from forming beads and rolling off the waxy leaf surface.
5.  **Test a Small Area**: Before spraying your whole field or greenhouse, test the mix on 2-3 leaves and wait 24 hours.

---

## Avoiding Biuret Toxicity

Biuret is a chemical byproduct sometimes found in commercial urea. While harmless in the soil, biuret is **Toxic to Leaves**.

When buying urea for foliar use, look for **"Low Biuret" Urea** (typically less than 0.5% biuret content). Standard agricultural urea can sometimes have 1-2% biuret, which is fine for soil but risky for leaves.

---

## Summary

Foliar spraying is a powerful tool for the modern grower. By keeping your concentrations between 5g and 10g per liter and spraying during the cool parts of the day, you can see a visible greening effect within 48 hours.
