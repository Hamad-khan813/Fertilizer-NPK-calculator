# How to Use Ferti-Calc for Custom Fertilizer Mixing

So you've got a target NPK ratio in your head and a shelf full of random fertilizer bags. Urea here, MOP over there, maybe some DAP you bought on sale last season. Now what? That's exactly where a fertilizer mixing calculator like Ferti-Calc earns its keep — instead of guessing and hoping your plants don't hate you for it.

This guide walks through custom fertilizer mixing using Ferti-Calc, step by step, whether you're blending a homemade NPK fertilizer mix for a vegetable patch or dialing in a precise nutrient solution for hydroponics.

## Why Custom Fertilizer Mixing Even Matters

Pre-mixed fertilizers are convenient. But they're also generic. A bag labeled 20-20-20 works fine as a "good enough" option — until your soil test says you need way more potassium than nitrogen, or your crop is in a growth stage that wants almost zero phosphorus. That's when custom fertilizer mixing (sometimes called fertilizer blending or DIY NPK mixing) starts to actually save you money and results, instead of just buying five different bags and eyeballing it.

A fertilizer ratio calculator takes the guesswork out. You tell it what you want, it tells you how much of each fertilizer to use.

## Step 1: Know Your Target NPK Ratio

Before opening Ferti-Calc, figure out your target N-P-K numbers. This usually comes from:

- A soil test report
- Crop-specific fertilizer recommendations (tomatoes want something different than lawns, obviously)
- A hydroponic nutrient schedule if you're growing indoors

Write down your target N%, P%, and K% — that's what you'll plug into the calculator.

## Step 2: Enter Your Fertilizers One at a Time

Right now, Ferti-Calc's dilution calculator works on a single fertilizer at a time — plug in the NPK analysis (like 46-0-0 for urea, or 0-0-60 for MOP), your target percentage, and your water or soil volume. Hit calculate, and it spits out exactly how many grams (or ml, for liquids) you need.

For a custom mix, you run this once per fertilizer:

1. Calculate the amount of your nitrogen source (urea, ammonium sulfate, whatever's in your shed) needed to hit your target N%.
2. Calculate your phosphorus source (like DAP or MAP) separately to hit target P%. Keep in mind DAP also contributes nitrogen, so subtract that from what your nitrogen source still needs to supply.
3. Calculate your potassium source (usually MOP or SOP) to hit target K%.

Yes, this takes a bit more math than a one-click "mix calculator" would. Real talk — that combined blend calculator is on our roadmap, so keep an eye out.

## Step 3: Check the Resulting NPK

After you've worked out your amounts, use the "resulting NPK" side of the calculator to double check what you're actually delivering. Enter the amount you plan to apply, the fertilizer's NPK percentage, and your volume — it'll show you the real N, P, and K percentage you'll end up with. This is the step most people skip, and it's the one that catches math errors before they hit your plants.

## Step 4: Watch for Binding Constraints

Here's the part that trips people up. If you're using a straight nitrogen fertilizer to hit your N target, it might also be quietly overshooting or undershooting on P and K depending on what else is in the blend. Always run the numbers for the nutrient that's hardest to hit — your "binding constraint" — first, then adjust the others around it.

## A Quick Example

Say your target is 10-5-10 for a vegetable bed, in a 20-litre mix.

- Urea (46-0-0) covers your nitrogen.
- DAP (18-46-0) covers phosphorus, but also adds nitrogen — so recalculate your urea amount downward once DAP's in.
- MOP (0-0-60) covers potassium.

Run each through Ferti-Calc, cross-check the totals with the resulting NPK tool, and you've got a custom fertilizer mix built for your exact numbers instead of whatever ratio happened to be on sale.

## Common Fertilizer Mixing Mistakes

- **Ignoring secondary nutrients from a source.** DAP isn't just phosphorus — it brings nitrogen along for the ride.
- **Mixing incompatible fertilizers.** Some combinations (calcium nitrate and any sulfate or phosphate fertilizer, for instance) can cause precipitation in solution. Always check compatibility before combining in liquid form.
- **Skipping the verification step.** Calculating the "amount needed" without checking "resulting NPK" is how small rounding errors turn into a lopsided feed.

## Final Thoughts

Custom fertilizer mixing with Ferti-Calc isn't complicated once you break it into per-fertilizer steps: figure out your target ratio, calculate each fertilizer's contribution, verify the resulting NPK, and adjust for overlap. It's a bit more hands-on than an all-in-one blend calculator, but it gets you a fertilizer mix that's actually built around your numbers — not just whatever bag was on the shelf.
