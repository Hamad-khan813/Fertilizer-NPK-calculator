# Urea vs Ammonium Nitrate: Choosing Your Nitrogen Source

Nitrogen is often the limiting nutrient in crop production, so choosing the right nitrogen fertilizer is critical to profitability. The two most common granular nitrogen sources are **Urea (46-0-0)** and **Ammonium Nitrate (34-0-0)**. But which one should you use?

## Quick Comparison

| Factor | Urea | Ammonium Nitrate |
|--------|------|------------------|
| N Content | 46% | 34% |
| Cost per Kg N | Lower | Higher |
| Volatilization Risk | High (if not incorporated) | Very low |
| Soil Acidification | Neutral to alkaline | Acidifying |
| Application Timing | More flexible | Any time |
| Storage | Very stable | Regulated, hygroscopic |

## Nitrogen Content & Application Rate

**Urea (46-0-0)** contains 46% nitrogen, while **Ammonium Nitrate (34-0-0)** contains only 34%. This means you need less urea by weight to deliver the same amount of nitrogen. If your target is 100 kg/ha of nitrogen:

- **Urea**: 100 ÷ 0.46 = 217 kg/ha
- **Ammonium Nitrate**: 100 ÷ 0.34 = 294 kg/ha

For transport and storage, urea is more efficient.

## Cost Efficiency

Urea is almost always cheaper per tonne, but you must compare cost per unit of nitrogen. Due to its higher N content, urea typically costs **15-25% less per kilogram of N** delivered.

**Trade-off**: If you don't incorporate urea quickly, you lose nitrogen to volatilization, negating the cost advantage.

## Volatilization Risk

### Urea
Urea is susceptible to **ammonia volatilization**, especially when:
- Left on the soil surface
- Applied in warm weather
- Soil pH is high (>7)

**Potential loss**: 10-40% of applied nitrogen in unfavorable conditions.

**Solution**: Incorporate urea into the soil within 24-48 hours of application, or use a urease inhibitor coating.

### Ammonium Nitrate
Ammonium Nitrate has **minimal volatilization risk**. The nitrate form is highly stable and does not volatilize under any weather or soil condition. This makes it ideal for:
- Surface application (no incorporation required)
- Foliar spray applications
- Situations where timely incorporation is impossible

## Soil Acidification

**Urea**: Leaves neutral to alkaline residue; does not acidify soil.

**Ammonium Nitrate**: The ammonium component can gradually acidify soil over time. Monitor pH in fields receiving repeated ammonium nitrate applications.

## Application Timing & Flexibility

**Urea** requires more careful timing — you must incorporate it quickly to prevent loss. This limits your application window in rainy or cold seasons.

**Ammonium Nitrate** can be applied at any time with no urgency to incorporate. It's ideal for late-season top-dressing or emergency nitrogen applications.

## Which Fertilizer Should You Choose?

### Use Urea If:
- You have **irrigation** and can apply followed by watering to incorporate
- You're in a **cooler climate** where volatilization is low
- You have **acidic soil** (pH < 6.5)
- **Cost is the primary concern** and you have equipment/labor for incorporation
- You're **pre-plant** or **preplant** applying

### Use Ammonium Nitrate If:
- You're doing **top-dressing** (cannot incorporate)
- Weather is **hot and dry** (high volatilization risk with urea)
- You have **alkaline soil** (pH > 7.5)
- You need **maximum reliability** with no risk of nitrogen loss
- You're doing **late-season** applications
- You're applying **foliar nitrogen** (only ammonium nitrate is suitable)

## A Practical Example

**Scenario**: You're top-dressing wheat in mid-grain fill at 50 kg/ha N in late May with high daytime temperatures.

- **Urea**: Risk of losing 30-40% of nitrogen to volatilization. You'd need to apply 80-100 kg/ha urea to ensure 50 kg/ha reaches the plant, wasting money.
- **Ammonium Nitrate**: Can apply exactly 147 kg/ha and have confidence all nitrogen reaches the crop.

In this case, **Ammonium Nitrate is the better choice despite higher cost**, because you actually retain more nitrogen.

## The Bottom Line

- **For preplant/incorporation scenarios**: Urea wins on cost
- **For top-dressing and reliability**: Ammonium Nitrate wins
- **For maximum efficiency**: Consider soil type, weather, and incorporation ability before choosing

Your agronomist or extension specialist can help match the right nitrogen source to your specific farm situation, soil type, and climate. When in doubt, a blend of both (e.g., 60% urea, 40% ammonium nitrate) gives you the cost advantage of urea with the reliability of ammonium nitrate.

Ultimately, **the cheapest fertilizer is the one that actually reaches your crop**. Choose based on your ability to incorporate and your climate conditions, not cost alone.
